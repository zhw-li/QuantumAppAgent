# Slide Design

10 design rules, visual hierarchy guidelines, and counterintuitive design insights for academic presentations.

---

## 10 Design Rules

### 1. One idea per slide — no exceptions
If a slide communicates two ideas, split it. The audience processes one thing at a time.

### 2. ~1 minute per slide
20 slides for a 20-minute talk. If you have 40 slides for 20 minutes, you will either rush or skip — both are worse than cutting content.

### 3. Heading = main message, not topic label
Write "Our method reduces error by 40% on challenging scenes" not "Results." Claim-style titles let a skimming audience follow your story from titles alone.

### 4. Include only elements you will discuss
Every item on a slide that you do not mention is a distraction. If you won't talk about it, remove it. The audience will read things you skip and wonder why they're there.

### 5. Prefer figures and diagrams over text
Presentations are visual. A well-designed diagram communicates faster than bullet points. If you find yourself typing bullets, ask: "Can I show this instead of saying it?"

### 6. Max 6 elements per slide
Beyond 6 distinct visual elements (text blocks, figures, arrows, labels), cognitive overload begins. Count your elements — if more than 6, simplify.

### 7. High contrast, readable fonts
- Sans-serif fonts (Helvetica, Arial, Calibri, Source Sans)
- Minimum 24pt for body text
- High contrast between text and background
- Test readability from the back of a large room

### 8. Distribute multi-panel figures across slides
Paper figures often pack 6-8 panels into one figure. On slides, distribute them: one panel per slide with narration. The audience cannot parse a dense multi-panel figure while listening.

### 9. Credit every borrowed figure
On every slide that uses someone else's figure: "Image: Author et al., Year" in small text. This is both ethical and professional — the audience notices.

### 10. Design for the distracted
At any moment, half your audience is checking email or thinking about lunch. Your slide should make sense to someone who just looked up. If it requires 30 seconds of context to parse, simplify it.

## Visual Hierarchy

Consistent sizing signals importance and reduces cognitive load.

| Element | Recommended Size |
|---------|-----------------|
| Slide title | 36-44pt, bold |
| Body text | 24-28pt |
| Annotations / labels | 18-20pt minimum |
| Source credits | 14-16pt, light color |

**Absolute minimum for any text**: 18pt. If it's smaller, the audience cannot read it and it becomes noise.

### Color Usage

- Use a consistent color palette throughout (2-3 primary colors max)
- Reserve one highlight color for emphasis (e.g., your method in blue, baselines in gray)
- Avoid red-green-only distinctions (color blindness affects ~8% of males)
- Dark text on light background is generally more readable in well-lit rooms
- Light text on dark background works better in dim rooms

### Alignment and Spacing

- Align elements to a grid — unaligned elements look unprofessional
- Generous margins (at least 5% on each side)
- Consistent spacing between bullet points and between slides
- Use master slides / templates to enforce consistency

## Counterintuitive Design Insights

### Animations are usually harmful

Animations break PDF fallback (you often need PDF as backup), create accessibility issues, and most audiences find them more distracting than helpful. The exception: step-by-step build of a complex figure where each step adds one element. Even then, prefer multiple slides over animation — they're more reliable.

### Paper figures rarely work on slides

Paper figures are designed for 300 DPI print at 3-4 inches wide. Projected on screen, labels become unreadable, thin lines disappear, and fine details are lost. Redesign key figures for projection: thicker lines, larger labels, fewer details, higher contrast.

### White space is content

An uncrowded slide communicates confidence and clarity. The urge to fill every corner with content comes from insecurity. A slide with one figure and a clear title is more effective than a slide packed with three figures and tiny labels.

### Consistent templates > creative layouts

Per-slide creative layouts confuse the audience because they must re-learn the visual structure of each slide. Use master slides with consistent placement of title, content, and page number. Save creativity for the content, not the layout.

### Bullet points are a last resort

If your slide is a list of bullets, you are using your slides as a teleprompter. Ask: "Can I replace these bullets with a figure, diagram, table, or example?" Usually, yes.

## Technical Disaster Prevention

### Always save a PDF backup
PowerPoint/Keynote files can fail on unfamiliar machines. A PDF always works. Export before you leave for the venue.

### Prepare screenshot alternatives for every video
Videos fail: wrong codec, no audio, wrong display adapter. For every video, have a slide with a representative screenshot and a note about what the video shows.

### Test on the venue display if possible
Colors, contrast, and font rendering differ between your laptop screen and a projector. Arrive early if you can and test.

### Avoid system-specific fonts
Use widely available sans-serif fonts. If you use a custom font and present on a different machine, the system will substitute — often badly.

### Embed all media
Linked images and videos break when you move the file to a USB drive. Embed everything directly in the presentation file.

### Check aspect ratio
Know whether the venue uses 16:9 or 4:3. Designing for the wrong ratio means either black bars or cropped content.
