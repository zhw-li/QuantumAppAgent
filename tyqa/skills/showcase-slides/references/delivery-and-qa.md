# Delivery and Q&A

Rehearsal protocol, delivery principles, counterintuitive insights, and Q&A preparation for academic presentations.

---

## Rehearsal Protocol

### Three-Pass Rehearsal

**Pass 1: Alone, for timing and flow**
- Run through the entire talk with a timer
- Note which slides take too long — these need to be split or cut
- Identify where you stumble — these sections need clearer notes or simpler slides
- Record yourself if possible; watching playback reveals habits you can't feel in the moment

**Pass 2: In front of 1-2 colleagues, for clarity**
- Ask them to interrupt whenever something is unclear
- After the talk, ask: "What was the main takeaway?" If they can't state it in one sentence, your message is not clear enough
- Ask: "Which slide was most confusing?" Fix that one before anything else

**Pass 3: Simulate Q&A**
- Ask your colleagues to ask the hardest questions they can think of
- Practice answering concisely (under 60 seconds per answer)
- If a question stumps you, prepare a backup slide for it

### Timing Rules

- If your talk is 20 minutes, rehearse to fill 18 — buffer for nerves and audience reactions
- Time each section individually; if one section is over budget, cut content from that section (don't speed up)
- The most common mistake is spending too long on motivation and method, leaving 2 minutes for results — budget results time first, then work backwards

## Delivery Principles

### Voice and Pacing

- **Never read your slides aloud** — narrate what the slide does not show. The slide shows the figure; you explain the insight behind it
- **Speak slower than feels natural** — non-native speakers in the audience need processing time, and nervousness makes everyone speak faster
- **Pause after key points** — silence signals importance. A 2-second pause after "This is our main contribution" is more effective than any transition word
- **Vary your tone** — monotone delivery puts audiences to sleep regardless of content quality

### Body Language

- **Make eye contact with the audience**, not the screen — glance at your slides briefly, then turn back
- **Point at specific parts of figures** when discussing them — "This curve here" while pointing is clearer than "As you can see"
- **Stand still during key points** — pacing signals nervousness; stillness signals confidence
- **Face the audience when speaking** — talking to the screen means they hear your back, not your voice

### Transitions

- Explicitly signal section changes: "That covers our method. Now let's look at the results."
- Recap briefly when entering results: "Remember, our core idea was X. Here's what happens when we test it."
- Avoid generic transitions ("Moving on...") — use content-based transitions ("Now that we've seen the architecture, the natural question is: does it actually work?")

## Counterintuitive Delivery Insights

### Finishing 1-2 minutes early is better than running over

Q&A time shows confidence. Running overtime shows poor preparation. If you finish a 20-minute talk at 18 minutes, the audience respects your discipline. If you finish at 22, you've stolen someone else's time.

### Admitting "I don't know" in Q&A builds credibility

Making up an answer destroys it. "That's a great question — I haven't tested that specific scenario, but my intuition is X because Y. I'd be happy to discuss after the session." This is vastly better than a confident but wrong answer.

### The audience forgets 90% of your talk

Design for the 10% you want them to remember. That 10% should be: (1) the problem you solve, (2) your key insight, and (3) your main result. Everything else is supporting material.

### Questions are not attacks

They mean the audience is engaged. Treat every question as a gift — even hostile-sounding ones. "Why didn't you compare with X?" means "I'm interested enough to think critically about your work." Respond with gratitude, not defensiveness.

### Nerves improve performance

Moderate anxiety sharpens focus and energy. The goal is not to eliminate nerves but to channel them. Rehearsal converts nervous energy from panic into enthusiasm.

## Q&A Preparation

### Prepare 5-10 Backup Slides

Place them after your "Thank You" slide. For each anticipated question, have a slide ready:

- **Full quantitative comparison table** — for "How does it compare to X?" questions
- **Failure case examples** — shows honesty, preparation, and understanding of limitations
- **Additional ablations** — for "What happens if you remove Y?" questions
- **Computational cost / runtime comparison** — nearly always asked
- **Broader impact / limitations** — common for top venues

### Anticipate Questions Systematically

For each contribution, ask:
- "What would a skeptic challenge?"
- "What is the most obvious follow-up question?"
- "What comparison or experiment would a reviewer request?"

For each related work you cite, ask:
- "What if someone asks why we didn't use their approach?"
- "What if the author of that paper is in the audience?"

### Answering Technique

1. **Pause before answering** (2-3 seconds) — shows you are thinking, not reacting
2. **Restate the question briefly** — confirms you understood and gives the audience context
3. **Answer the core question first** (1-2 sentences), then elaborate if needed
4. **Keep answers under 60 seconds** — long answers monopolize Q&A time and lose the audience
5. **If you need a backup slide**, navigate to it calmly — no need to apologize for being prepared

### Handling Difficult Situations

- **A question you cannot answer**: "I don't have that data, but I'd be happy to follow up by email."
- **A hostile questioner**: Stay calm, respond to the substance, ignore the tone. The audience judges the questioner's behavior, not yours.
- **A long, rambling question**: Wait for a pause, then say: "If I understand correctly, your question is about X — is that right?" This refocuses the exchange.
- **No questions at all**: Don't panic. Say "I'm happy to discuss further during the break" and step down gracefully.

## Day-of Logistics

- [ ] Arrive early to test equipment (display, audio, pointer/clicker)
- [ ] Bring your own display adapter (HDMI, USB-C, both)
- [ ] Have slides on USB + cloud + email (triple backup)
- [ ] Know the room layout and how the pointer/clicker works
- [ ] Confirm your time slot and duration with the session chair
- [ ] Have water available (dry mouth from nerves is real)
- [ ] Close all notifications on your laptop before presenting
- [ ] If using your own laptop, plug in the charger (battery dies at the worst moment)
