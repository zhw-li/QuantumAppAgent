# Writing Practice

Paper writing ability improves through deliberate practice, not by writing more words. This guide provides three progressive training paths for developing the skills needed to write clear, compelling papers.

## Three Training Paths

### 1. Daily Paper Summaries

Read one paper per day and write a structured summary:
- One sentence: the paper's novelty (what is genuinely new?)
- A few sentences: the pipeline (Step 1 does X, Step 2 does Y, Step 3 does Z)

This trains two critical writing skills:
- **Summarization**: Distilling complex methods into concise descriptions — the same skill needed for abstracts, introductions, and related work
- **Logical expression**: Articulating WHY something works, not just WHAT it does — the skill needed for method sections and analysis

Use the paper summary template from the `research-ideation` skill for a more comprehensive format.

### 2. Verbal Articulation Practice

In daily interactions (meetings, hallway conversations, messages), practice clearly describing:
- Your current idea and its contributions
- Your progress and what's blocking you
- Your questions and what you need help with

**The test**: If you can't explain your work clearly in conversation, you can't write it clearly in a paper. Verbal practice is faster feedback than writing practice — you immediately see whether the listener understands.

### 3. Presentation as Writing Practice

Making slides with clear logical flow trains the same narrative skills needed for paper writing:
- Selecting what to include vs. omit (scope decisions)
- Ordering information for maximum clarity (narrative structure)
- Explaining complex ideas simply (audience awareness)

See the `academic-slides` skill for presentation-specific guidance.

## Counterintuitive Writing Insights

- **Writing ability improves through summarization, not through writing more words.** The bottleneck is clear thinking, not fluent prose. Practice compressing ideas, not expanding them.
- **The Introduction's first 2-3 paragraphs are the hardest part.** They require deep understanding of the field's problems and why existing approaches fall short — not just knowledge of your own solution. If these paragraphs are weak, the rest of the paper suffers regardless of quality.
- **If your method section is hard to write, your pipeline isn't clear enough.** Difficulty in writing usually signals confusion in thinking. Go back to planning and clarify the method design before continuing to write.
